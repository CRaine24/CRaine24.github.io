Writing

Purring

Sometimes, I still find bottle caps and broken rubber bands under the living room chairs when I clean. Her claws would scrape against the wooden floor as she chased her toys or carried them out of my reach. I’d click my tongue, hold out my hand and rub my fingers together—come here, bring me your toy, in that voice an octave higher than usual. Sometimes, I pull a strand of dark brown fur from my pillowcase and remember how my bedroom door would open around eleven o’clock each night, letting in a sliver of light from the hallway, and I’d call to her and feel her weight land on the foot of my bed and her paws press against my legs and her soft body collapse beside my head. The warm rumbling against my cheek. Motorboat. She made that noise during her last vet visit, too, but it was different somehow. Sometimes, I turn between my fingers the thread she pried up from my comforter during our playtime—I’d run my hand under the blankets, the pupils of her green eyes dilated to the size of full moons as she watched the movement, get it, she’d wiggle and pounce, I’d laugh and now I can only manage a smile when I think of it. I tell myself she’s happier now. Somewhere, I imagine, she’s purring and chasing whatever small thing isn’t tied down and jumping at fingers under blankets. Angels, let her bat at your wings, I trimmed her claws not that long before she joined you.
