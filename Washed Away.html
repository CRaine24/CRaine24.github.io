Writing

Washed Away

The sky is always gray here at the edge of the cliff, the wind constant and cold and wet, but I don’t mind. It’s an isolated place. Peaceful. Sound carries far but reaches no one. Town is miles away—just meadowlands all around me. I’ll often come here to read, or to sketch the horizon. I remember how, when I first began this practice two years ago, it felt so strange to be alone by choice. To do something for my own pleasure. It had been almost twenty years since I’d had the chance.

I’ve been coming here more and more recently. My house, sitting atop the hill behind the cliff, is small, stifling, lonely. Even lonelier than when my husband lived there with me. But at least it’s quieter now. I’m sure I would move into town if I had the money, if only to escape the memories in the broken bedroom mirror and the faded bloodstains on the carpet, but after everything that’s happened I can hardly be bothered by a powerless phantom. I hear the wind blowing at night and smile as I imagine it being a ghost too weak to come through the door.

Sometimes I stand with my toes at the very edge of the bluff and lean over as far as I dare to look down at the coastline. I search for something I know I’ll never see and find, as expected, only the waves crashing against the rocks.

Darling husband. I want to know if you’re still in the same place you landed after you tripped and fell. Pity you were always so unstable.

Has the saltwater ruined your suit? Have the mermaids taken your necktie? Has the ocean worn you to bone? Poor dear.
